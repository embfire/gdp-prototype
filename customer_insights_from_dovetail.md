Across all interviews, customers consistently cited six thematic areas of analytics importance. Here's a breakdown with direct quotes where available:

1. 🔄 Retention & Guest Lifecycle
This is the single most prominent theme. Customers want to understand at what point guests drop off — and how to intervene.

New guest return rate was called a "powerful metric" and a revelation. "Having a baseline, literally understanding what is the baseline of our new guest return rate — we did not have that until Vicki, and I think that it's a powerful metric. In Punch we have it for a loyalty guest, but we know that most of our loyalty guests are not new guests."[1]

First-to-third order conversion was flagged as a key stickiness indicator. Corey noted: "The drop off rates are pretty high in often cases — various brands I've worked for — and that punch metric is quite interesting. If you get a guest sticky on a transaction at least three times, they actually remain sticky, which is the ultimate goal: driving frequency."[2]

Lapse detection thresholds matter greatly. Maddie (Luna Grill) said: "I think of just like them lapsing, you know, no purchases within 90 days. Once you're beyond 90 days, we're a little concerned. After about 120, I'm really sweating, and six months is where I'm like, okay, I think this might be a lot harder to recover this guest."[3]

Time between visits is a key early warning signal. April (Charley's) explained: "When I was looking at mParticle, we could see time between visits pretty clearly. So like if their first to second purchase was 10 days, their second to third purchase was 25 days, and then their third to fourth was 80 days — that would be an indicator to me that we're not trending in the right direction."[4]

2. 💰 Revenue & Spend Metrics
Customers want granular spend visibility at the guest and segment level — not just aggregate totals.

Average check size / AOV is a core metric used for segmentation and campaign targeting. April (Charley's) described her ideal guest profile view: "If I could go to a guest profile and I could see their average days between visits, what their annual spend is, what their average check is — things like that. And then I could segment based on those and find other customers that were like this one — that would be valuable."[5]

Incrementality was cited by Zaxby's as the most important macro metric. Andrew Hyde (Zaxby's) said: "Everything is around incrementality. Is the program overall incremental? Is the offers incremental? Are we getting them engaged more? I think integrating it more with our full purchase feed and really being able to do a snapshot of incrementality for the program — are we driving more incremental visits, incremental checks, sales compared to non-loyalty guests — that's the top most important thing."[6]

LTV (lifetime value) of loyalty vs. non-loyalty guests was a live request at the board level. Eugene (PAR) shared: "Our CMO texted me for the board meeting: 'What is the most up-to-date LTV for loyalty and non-loyalty?' That's easy because in Biki I just used the same segment and toggled back and forth."[7]

Revenue and total orders over time were called "mind-blowingly helpful." Evan (Condado Tacos) noted trend reports tracking "revenue, total orders, new guest orders over very large periods of time — those were questions that I had that when we first onboarded, those reports weren't available, and now they are, and it's like mind-blowingly helpful."[8][9][10]

3. 👥 Guest Behavior & Engagement
Customers want behavioral signals — especially for non-loyalty guests — that go beyond simple transaction counts.

App opens and purchase timing are critical early indicators for Zaxby's. Andrew Hyde (Zaxby's) explained: "We look at that seven-day window and see that 95% of the people that go on to make a purchase do so in the first day. We saw that if you download the app, 95% of those people who make a purchase also open the app that first day. So we started to be able to see all these clear indicators — if you download the app and didn't come back within a day or two, you're likely gone."[11]

Visit frequency by segment and understanding anonymous vs. loyalty guest behavior were foundational asks. Evan (Condado Tacos) described their foundational questions as: "New guest count, return rate of new guests, engaged guest percentage, and then the whole can of worms of the value of loyalty — which is hard to actually quantify in Punch. We know the value of a loyalty guest, but we don't really know the value of an anonymous guest."[12]

Channel ordering behavior was mentioned as a gap. Customers want to know how guests order across channels (in-store, online, third-party) and track average spend across them. One customer noted they want to understand "what maybe that average would be over time for them to order across multiple channels, because right now with third party orders we don't get a lot of customer data there."[13]

Session behavior (app opens without purchase) is a missing data point. Joanna (Zaxby's) flagged: "More so like a session abandonment — if they start a session, they're always opening the app but never placing an order. We sometimes talk to those folks differently than people who do open the app."[14]

4. 🍽️ Menu & Product Affinity
Menu reporting emerged as a surprisingly high-value data category across multiple brands.

Item-level reorder rate and return rate drove real business decisions at Condado Tacos. Evan (Condado Tacos) shared: "The Chicken Bacon Ranch taco has a huge reorder rate for existing guests and has a higher return rate than the average product for new guests. So we were looking at what would be our first LTO burrito and we're like — it's a no brainer, it's obviously the Chicken Bacon Ranch needs to be a burrito."[15]

Menu reporting was cited as the fastest path to value when onboarding a new analytics platform, and was used by culinary, operations, marketing, and finance. Evan (Condado Tacos) noted: "Marketing, FP&A, operations actively uses Biki, and then culinary uses Biki for the menu reporting."[16]

Item affinity segmentation — the ability to segment guests by what they order and build campaigns around that — was a clear wish. Maddie (Luna Grill) explained: "We want to create a segment of users and then be able to see those behaviors from just a specific segment — like if we could create users who are only coming for the kids-eat-free offer, what do the products purchased look like for that segment versus the guest who only orders online or orders several entrees at a time? Digging a little bit deeper there would be really exciting."[17]

5. 📍 Location & Operational Performance
Customers track performance not just at the brand level, but at the market and individual location level.

Location-level retention and term performance are actively monitored. Evan (Condado Tacos) noted: "Operations is looking at the performance of retention performance, at-term performance by location and by market. Now there's location-based reporting in Biki, which is incredibly helpful."[18]

New guest return rate by location was a key metric for market managers. Evan shared that market managers "go into the location reports quite a bit — looking at each of their locations, comparing to each other, looking at new guest return rate. And that's really the metric that we're looking at as a brand and studying the health of it."[19]

Question
What pain points do our customers mention about analytics or reporting? What do they say is hard to measure, takes too long, or requires manual work?

Six recurring pain points emerge across all interviews, ranging from data latency to tool complexity and manual workarounds.

1. ⏱️ Report Generation is Too Slow
The most viscerally expressed pain point. Customers are waiting unacceptably long for data to return.

"One of our biggest challenges with Punch is the time it takes to generate data. We go, put a segment together, pull it together, run a report, get some data — sometimes we're waiting a long time. Waiting 30 minutes for a report, even though it might be really cool, is very challenging."[1]

The sentiment was direct: "Today this is kind of pain — I would recommend when we build this, we find a way to make this more performant. Because if this tool is about delivering insights, I can't be waiting 30 minutes and that's what I have to do today."[2]

2. 🔒 Pre-Canned Reports with No Flexibility
Customers feel trapped by fixed dashboard views and can't slice data the way they need to.

Joanna (Zaxby's) put it clearly: "The punch dashboard today is, I would say, a little bit limiting. There's a lot of sort of pre-canned reports that you can do, but you can't maybe kind of add or look by different KPIs."[3]

Andrew Hyde (Zaxby's) echoed this: "This is one thing that the current Punch iteration doesn't have a great way of doing — it tells us a lot of just 'yesterday you had this many people who did this.'"[4]

Raymond (PAG) added that static snapshots are the core problem: "I find static screenshots of data points in time to be a little bit less helpful — it's only helpful if we're able to assess how guest visits, for example, is impacted as a result of those initiatives. You need to be able to track the changes in time, not just to be able to say, last week your data was this and this week your data is that."[5]

3. 🧩 No Visibility Into Non-Loyalty Guests
A fundamental and widespread gap — customers can't see or reach the majority of their guests.

One customer noted: "We do value our Punch system to give us data for loyalty members, but we do not understand our non-loyalty members — which is over 80% of sales."[6]

The operational impact was described bluntly: "It's been very stressful. We do bring in the data, we could do our own crunching — but then you're totally depending on that API, and sometimes the API is not available that morning. We don't have the information as far as their phone numbers or email addresses. We can't even reach out to the majority of our guests. What we're really trying to strive is to reach out to those that come to our location quite frequently but just refuse to be a loyalty member — and that's where we're really struggling."[7]

4. 🛠️ Manual Work and API Dependency
Customers are doing their own data crunching because the tools don't surface insights proactively — and that work is fragile.

April (Charley's) described the reality: "We are doing a lot of kind of reactive work — the most ideal scenario is that we would have some sort of data platform that could help us be more proactive and help us identify opportunity segments or what offers are going to best go with a certain segment. We're starting to work on that at CKE through some work with Ernst and Young where they were doing machine learning stuff — the whole idea was that it was supposed to be much more proactive and not just always, constantly trying to find data that will answer a question coming at you from leadership."[8]

At Condado Tacos, Evan described a workaround that adds significant manual overhead: "Some of that is screenshots and downloads of Biki, some of it is taking that data and reformatting it for a different audience. For the board, we would typically not use a screenshot — we would make that our own. I can tell you I've just started using Claude to help me make better visualizations of more complex data and interactive data."[9]

5. 🤖 AI Tools Produce Unreliable Results for Non-Technical Users
Where AI analytics tools exist, the results can mislead non-expert users.

One PAG participant flagged a real problem: "We use an AI chatbot with some of our data, and part of what we run into is that the people who are prompting aren't data scientists. And so they don't ask the right questions. And as a result, they'll get very strange answers."[10]

The consequences weren't trivial: "Without statistical background knowledge, they request information like aggregated sales data for a group of locations, and they don't frame it right — and it'll give them incorrect data or correct data, but incorrectly formulated. And that has caused problems for us."[11]

This was reinforced in feedback from another participant: "Unreliable for junior team members who aren't data scientists — want this tool to be easy enough for them to use and start helping with."[12]

6. 🔗 Identity Resolution & Cross-Platform Data Integrity
Stitching data across platforms is a persistent, time-consuming, and error-prone problem.

Andrew Hyde (Zaxby's) described the data complexity: "Right now we have Braze data mixing with mParticle data, mixing with Punch data. We have survey data coming in. We have all these data feeds that a lot of times..."[13][14]

Joanna (Zaxby's) described the fragility of the logic underpinning it: "We have documents upon documents on how the logic for our ID resolution is set up. And sometimes that doesn't work. There have been times where after six months we kind of had to throw that out — were we resolving based off email first? Based off credit card first? Each of them has pros and cons and we do it one way and then find, oh, that's not quite doing what we needed to do."[15]

On the campaign data side, Zaxby's identified a specific lag issue: "One thing that's a little bit tricky is the data on the dashboard is real-time, but the campaign data isn't real-time. We'll go into the log to be like, okay, we knew 5 million people are supposed to get this campaign — we'll go into the log and see that 5,000 people were gifted that. Just having a very clear 'X people were targeted, here's how many we processed the campaign to today' — so you're not having to dig through logs to find that out."[16]

7. 🔍 Segment-Level Granularity Is Missing
Customers want to analyze behavior within a specific segment, not just across the whole program — and today's tools make that difficult.

Maddie Nixon (Luna Grill) described the struggle: "Where I struggle with answering some of those questions is with the granularity — I can go and click into individual guest profiles, but it's hard for me to then sum up all those into an archetype succinctly."[17]

She elaborated on what she'd want instead: "With Punch, we can leverage receipt tags, but sometimes it feels like we want to do it the opposite way — we want to create a segment of users and then be able to see those behaviors from just a specific segment, versus 'here are your most sold items for the whole program.' Like, if we could create users who are only coming for the kids-eat-free offer — what do the products purchased look like for that segment versus the guest who only orders online? Digging a little deeper there would be really exciting."[18]

Maddie (Luna Grill) described her instinct the same way: "I think questions about frequency — we want to understand a lot about who generally our guests are and what type of guests they are. The other 50% of profile lookups at Zaxby's are about data integrity checks: "The other 50% of the time looking at a guest profile is to ensure data is being processed. If we had a visual that could send out and we could just report on loyalty sales or all the guests as the Guest360 product, that would be really helpful — just clear visuals that can then be shared with key stakeholders who maybe don't want to get into the nitty gritty of Excel files."[12]

What do customers say about guest retention, repeat visits, or churn? How important is it to them compared to other metrics?

Guest retention is universally treated as the most critical metric category across every brand interviewed — it anchors campaign strategy, informs daily workflow, and is the lens through which customers evaluate program health. Here's the full picture:

1. 🔄 Retention is the #1 Brand Health Metric
At Condado Tacos, new guest return rate is treated as the single most important signal of brand health. Market managers go into location reports quite a bit, looking at each of their locations, comparing to each other, and looking at new guest return rate — "that's really the metric that we're looking at as a brand and studying the health of it."[1]

At Zaxby's, the definition of health is framed around incrementality and re-engagement. Andrew Hyde described wanting a dashboard that shows "how many guests have purchased in the last 60 days — and also like how many people yesterday, out of say 50,000 check-ins, how many of those were re-engaged that haven't been active in the last 60 days."[2]

2. 📉 Lapse Thresholds Are Brand-Specific but Universally Watched
Every brand has a defined mental model of when a guest is "in trouble" — and they operate tiered intervention campaigns around it.

Bobby (Graeter's) described a structured 30/60/90-day recall ladder: "After 90 days, I would say you start to fall into like, okay, you're not thinking about Graeter's anymore — we're not top of mind. So after that 90 days, if you're not coming back in, typically we want to hit that person again. If a guest hasn't come in in six months, that's a 'hey, we really need to intervene' and send them something really enticing. And then lastly we have a last ditch effort where we do reach out at that year mark, right before their account becomes inactive, trying one more time to win them back — just knowing our guests have such a wide range of how often they come back."[3]

Maddie (Luna Grill) runs a similar model around 120 and 180 days: "Next month we want to do a B test to see which offers will work most effectively for guests who have lapsed 120 and 180 days."[4]

3. 🏁 First-to-Third Visit Conversion is the Stickiness Threshold
Multiple customers cite the third visit as the moment a guest becomes truly retained — not just acquired.

Corey (PAG Kickoff) put it directly: "If you get a guest sticky on a transaction at least three times, they actually remain sticky — which is the ultimate goal, driving frequency. That's been a pretty enlightening metric. It's something we strive for."[5][6]

Bobby (Graeter's) confirmed this pattern shaped their entire journey strategy: She looked at "how frequent does a guest come in and how long does it take to get them to their first, second, third, fourth, fifth visit — because that drove some decisions with our Journey to Third Visit campaign when we were first building out our loyalty program. And every year we audit those campaigns to make sure those offers are still working."[7]

4. ⏱️ Days Between Visits as an Early Warning Signal
April (Charley's) described using the pace of visits — not just lapse status — as a leading indicator of churn risk: "The average days between visits" is used as a lapsing definition — "if our average days between first and second visit is 23 days and it's been 24 days, then I'm going to consider you lapsing and try to give you something to come back. That's like the best info I have today."[8]

She also directly connected low loyalty frequency to a systemic program problem: "Right now our loyalty guest frequency is lower than what we would say our brand frequency is — and that's obviously a huge indicator of loyalty program opportunity if you know they aren't engaging in the program at a higher rate than you would expect them to based on just like an average guest."[9]

5. 📲 Early Behavioral Signals (Pre-Churn)
Zaxby's goes further than any other brand in tracking behavioral signals before a guest churns — using app session data to predict drop-off before it happens.

Andrew Hyde (Zaxby's) explained: "We look at that seven-day window and see that 95% of the people that go on to make a purchase do so in the first day. We saw that if you download the app, 95% of those people who make a purchase also open the app that first day. So we started to be able to see clear indicators — if you download the app and didn't come back within a day or two, you're likely gone. Just because we could see that fall-off, that very few people downloaded the app, signed up and then opened it on day five and made a purchase."[10]

6. 🏪 Store-Level Retention Drives Operational Motivation
Bobby (Graeter's) highlighted that surfacing retention data at the location level doesn't just serve marketing — it motivates frontline managers: "We're in a world where transaction count is down. But it's very encouraging when we are looking at redemption rates and participation rate and check-ins — our managers feel more motivated and excited to talk about the loyalty program. It's a good way for them to understand that our loyalty guests come in more often and spend more money. So that motivates them to talk about the loyalty program and convert non-loyalty guests to loyalty guests."[11]

7. 🤖 The Personalization Gap in Win-Back Campaigns
Where retention strategy breaks down for most brands is in knowing how to win back a lapsing guest — not just that they've lapsed. Maddie (Luna Grill) captured the gap clearly: "It would be really helpful if I knew more about activating around receipt tags and trying to figure out what in this guest's purchase history could be the right offer on the right product to recall them. Because we aren't picking up on that with our current resources, we do a lot of broad sweeps, trying to guess or figure out where they might be — and assume, okay, maybe this is the right point to offer them a super personalized bowl versus discounting a family meal."[12]

Bottom line: Retention is not one metric — it's an entire operating model for these brands. The gaps they feel most acutely are: (1) not knowing why a guest lapsed, (2) not having behavioral data to personalize the win-back, and (3) not being able to track non-loyalty guests through the same retention lens at all.

Question

Do customers talk about menu item performance or how menu items impact guest behavior?

Menu analytics is one of the highest-signal, most actionable topics across the interviews — and Condado Tacos stands out as the brand that has gone deepest on it. Here's the full picture:

1. 🌮 Menu Reporting as Fastest Time to Value
Condado Tacos (Evan) is the clearest example of a brand that unlocked immediate business value from menu-level analytics — and it happened faster than any other reporting category.

"The fastest time to value was the menu reporting because we very quickly saw a few things were happening with specific items." Evan described the moment of discovery: "We get into Biki, we're like, whoa, the Chicken Bacon Ranch taco has a huge reorder rate for existing guests and has a higher return rate than the average product for new guests." The business impact was direct and decisive: "So we were looking at what would be our first LTO burrito and we're like, well, it's a no brainer — it's obviously the Chicken Bacon Ranch needs to be a burrito. And so we launched the Chicken Bacon Ranch burrito and it was one of our most successful LTOs."

2. 🍽️ Menu Data Drives Cross-Functional Decisions
At Condado Tacos, menu reporting isn't siloed in marketing — it's used across four distinct teams.

Evan described the cross-functional reach: "Marketing, FP&A, operations actively uses Biki, and then culinary uses Biki for the menu reporting." This is notable because culinary teams are typically the last to adopt data tools — yet menu item performance data pulled them in organically.

3. 🔁 Reorder Rate and Return Rate as Item-Level KPIs
Two specific item-level metrics emerged as the most actionable for menu decisions:

Reorder rate — how often existing guests order the same item again

Return rate — whether a specific item drives new guests to come back

The Chicken Bacon Ranch example demonstrated both working together: high reorder rate from existing guests confirmed loyalty to the item, while high return rate among new guests confirmed it was a discovery driver — making it the perfect LTO candidate.

4. 🧩 Segment-Level Menu Behavior is the Next Frontier
Luna Grill's Maddie Nixon described the most sophisticated version of what customers want from menu analytics — the ability to overlay segment identity onto item-level behavior, not just look at program-wide item performance.

"With Punch, we can leverage receipt tags, but sometimes it feels like we want to do it the opposite way — we want to create a segment of users and then be able to see those behaviors from just a specific segment, versus 'here are your most sold items for the whole program.' Like, if we could create users who are only coming for the kids-eat-free offer — what do the products purchased look like for that segment versus the guest who only orders online or orders several entrees at a time? Digging a little bit deeper there would be really exciting." This is a clear product gap: customers want item affinity by segment, not just aggregate item popularity.

5. 🏷️ Receipt Tags as a Proxy for Menu Intelligence
Luna Grill is using receipt tags as a workaround to approximate item-level behavioral data inside Punch — but it's directional at best.

Maddie described the intent: "It would be really helpful if I knew more about activating around receipt tags and trying to figure out what in this guest's purchase history could be the right offer on the right product to recall them." The limitation is that this requires knowing what to tag upfront, and the insight only flows one way — you can filter by tag, but you can't discover unknown patterns. The "opposite way" Maddie describes (segment first, then see what they bought) is what's missing.

6. 🎯 Menu Affinity as a Personalization Engine for Win-Back
The most forward-looking use of menu data mentioned across the interviews is using item purchase history to personalize win-back and retention campaigns — moving away from broad discount sweeps toward offer-item matching.

Maddie (Luna Grill) captured the aspiration: "We aren't picking up on that with our current resources, we do a lot of broad sweeps, trying to guess or figure out where they might be — and assume, okay, maybe this is the right point to offer them a super personalized bowl versus discounting a family meal. It would be really helpful if I knew more about activating around receipt tags and trying to figure out what in this guest's purchase history could be the right offer on the right product to recall them." The implication is clear: menu affinity data, if surfaced correctly, becomes the engine for 1:1 personalization at scale.

7. 🔍 LTO Performance Tracking
Bobby (Graeter's) mentioned tracking LTO and promotional performance as part of their regular audit cycle: "Every year we audit those campaigns to make sure those offers are still working." While not explicitly tied to item-level data, the audit cadence implies that offer-item pairing effectiveness is being evaluated — even if the tooling to do it precisely isn't fully in place.

Bottom line: Menu analytics is underutilized but extremely high-value. Condado Tacos proved the ROI in a single LTO cycle. The gap most brands feel is moving from program-level item rankings to segment-level item affinity — knowing not just what sells, but what specific guest types buy, reorder, and return for.

Question

What do customers say about comparing performance across locations or stores?

Location-level comparisons are an active and emotionally significant part of how customers use data — spanning operational decisions, franchisee motivation, and brand health monitoring. Four distinct themes emerge:

1. 📍 New Guest Return Rate is the Primary Location-Level KPI
At Condado Tacos, location reporting is built around a single headline metric. Operations looks at retention performance and at-term performance by location and by market — and location-based reporting in Bikky is described as "incredibly helpful."[1] Market managers all have access and go into the location reports quite a bit, looking at each of their locations, comparing to each other, and looking at new guest return rate — "that's really the metric that we're looking at as a brand and studying the health of it."[2][3]

2. 🏆 Location Scoreboards as a Motivational Tool
At Graeter's, location-level data isn't just an analytics exercise — it's a frontline management tool. Showing store teams the loyalty scoreboards and how their store is doing in that data was "a great positive thing to talk about to help keep them motivated and help keep their teams talking about the loyalty program, because it's what's driving in the traffic — so even though we may have not saw an increase in our transactions, we did see an increase in our loyalty sales."[4]

When asked whether stores use it competitively, Bobby (Graeter's) confirmed the dynamic is real: some managers are very competitive, loyalty is one of the KPI items tracked, some markets are more competitive than others, and some of it is beating against your best self [5].

The most striking example was the airport location: the Cincinnati airport store is the lowest-scoring location — it doesn't get a lot of returning guests, mostly one-offs, with regulars being frequent pilots, flight attendants, and airport workers. Despite this structural disadvantage, "they were very excited that they went from the lowest spot to the third lowest — they jumped two spots on that score, and they had so much pride in just growing their loyalty program because they worked really hard at it."[6][7][8]

3. 🔎 Location Filtering as a Segmentation Dimension
Multiple customers flag location — and location group — as a fundamental filter for any meaningful analytics. Raymond noted that everything from typical RFM to time ranges to location ranges is essential — "time and location are obviously key for orders."[9][10]

In the PAG Room 2 session, the Guest360 segment builder prototype was specifically shown using location group as a primary example filter, noting that location group is "usually a little bit more relevant," and that lifecycle stage definitions will adjust over time to make sure they fit what works for each business [11].

4. 📊 What Customers Want But Don't Have: Trends Over Time by Location
The most common frustration is that location-level data is presented as static point-in-time snapshots rather than trend lines. One PAG participant pushed hard on this: the need is to assess how guest visits are impacted as a result of initiatives, and "you need to be able to track the changes in time, not just to be able to say, last week your data was this and this week your data is that — it's a lot easier to be able to see unique visits over time and to have flexibility to adjust the dates and have all the associated KPIs adjust accordingly."[12]

Another participant specifically called out the problem with default comparison periods: week-over-week is "not super relevant for us because of seasonality" — what's needed is the ability to see trends over time "and then even better compared to our targets or a baseline that's pertinent."[13]

5. 🤝 Democratizing Location Data to Franchisees
A recurring concern across PAG sessions is that location-level insights are too locked up in complex tools that franchisees can't easily access. One participant asked directly whether Guest360 reporting would reach franchisees: "is this part and parcel with overhauling some of the reporting in Punch and Power Engagement overall? Because there's basically a dashboard and a lot of Tableau views that are fairly difficult to use and then there's data exports — thinking about how we democratize this information to our franchisees and all those users."[14]

Bottom line: Customers want location-level data that is comparative (store vs. store, market vs. market), trend-based rather than point-in-time, and accessible enough for franchisee managers — not just corporate analytics teams. The emotional pull of location scoreboards also suggests that visibility into store-level performance isn't purely analytical; it's a meaningful motivational lever for the field.

Question

What do customers say about loyalty program analytics — enrollment rates, loyalty vs. non-loyalty differences, or loyalty ROI?

Loyalty analytics is the deepest and most emotionally charged topic across all interviews — customers are simultaneously most invested in it and most frustrated by the gaps in measuring it. Six major themes emerge:

1. 📈 Participation Rate as the North Star Metric
For Luna Grill, loyalty participation rate isn't just a KPI — it's the foundational business metric because Punch is their only CRM.

Maddie Nixon (Luna Grill) was explicit: "Loyalty has really become like the engine for our business. We're really big on participation rate, getting as many people in loyalty as we can. For us it's really because Punch is the only CRM platform we have — it's the only technical piece where we have any visibility on who our guests are." The implication is significant: for brands without a broader CDP or data warehouse, loyalty participation rate is a proxy for total guest visibility — not just program health.

2. 💰 Loyalty vs. Non-Loyalty Value Gap — The Board-Level Question
Quantifying the incremental value of a loyalty guest versus a non-loyalty guest is the single most common executive-level data request across brands.

Eugene (PAR) described a real-time board meeting scenario: "Our CMO texted me for the board meeting: 'What is the most up-to-date LTV for loyalty and non-loyalty?' That's easy because in Biki I just used the same segment and toggled back and forth." At Condado Tacos, this comparison is foundational to how they report program health: "Our CEO gets the weekly Biki report — it's just week over week performance of sales revenue per restaurant, new guests, re-engaged guests, re-onboarding guests. Those are the lifecycle stages of Biki. And so we're looking at, hey, we launched an LTO, we actually saw an influx of engaged guests — and that's regardless of loyalty or not." Evan elaborated on the challenge of truly isolating loyalty value: "We know the value of a loyalty guest, but we don't really know the value of an anonymous guest. So it's hard to actually quantify the value of loyalty in Punch."

3. 🔍 Incrementality — The Zaxby's Gold Standard
For Zaxby's, all loyalty analytics ultimately ladder up to one question: is the program driving incremental behavior?

Andrew Hyde (Zaxby's) stated it as their top priority: "Everything is around incrementality. Is the program overall incremental? Is the offers incremental? Are we getting them engaged more? I think integrating it more with our full purchase feed and really being able to do a snapshot of incrementality for the program — are we driving more incremental visits, incremental checks, sales compared to non-loyalty guests — that's the top most important thing." This is also why control groups are Zaxby's most-built audience in mParticle: "The number one thing we build in our current CDP, audience-wise, is around control groups. With testing — Control A, B, C, D — we generate that all in mParticle today and push outwards." Without proper control groups, incrementality claims are unprovable.

4. 📉 Active Rate Benchmarking — The Hidden Problem
Most brands are flying blind on what a "good" active rate actually looks like — and Punch's default definition doesn't match industry benchmarks.

Joanna (Zaxby's) described the gap directly: "Punch was comparing a lot to past year, and that is not industry best-in-class active. So we've worked really hard — we might not be 90 days like a Starbucks or a Dunkin, but really understanding what is our active rate and how can we continue to grow that." The absence of an industry benchmark forces brands to self-define activity thresholds — which means no two brands are measuring the same thing and cross-brand comparisons break down entirely.

5. 🚫 The Non-Loyalty Blind Spot — 80%+ of Guests Are Invisible
The most structurally damaging gap across all brands is the inability to measure, reach, or understand non-loyalty guests — who represent the majority of revenue for every brand interviewed.

One customer stated this plainly: "We do value our Punch system to give us data for loyalty members, but we do not understand our non-loyalty members — which is over 80% of sales." April (Charley's) described a specific consequence: "Right now our loyalty guest frequency is lower than what we would say our brand frequency is — and that's obviously a huge indicator of loyalty program opportunity if you know they aren't engaging in the program at a higher rate than you would expect them to based on just like an average guest." Without non-loyalty data, you can't even confirm your loyalty program is working as intended. Evan (Condado Tacos) described the analytical dead end: "We know the value of a loyalty guest, but we don't really know the value of an anonymous guest. So it's hard to actually quantify in Punch. We know the value of a loyalty guest, but we don't really know the value of an anonymous guest."

6. 🎯 Campaign-Level Loyalty ROI — The Missing Feedback Loop
Customers want to close the loop between a campaign sent and the loyalty behavior it drove — but today that feedback loop is either missing, delayed, or manual.

Maddie (Luna Grill) described the feature she envies in Attentive: "Something Attentive has — I really like when you send a campaign, you can see the segment performance." The implication is that Punch's campaign reporting doesn't give the same post-send visibility into how segments responded. Andrew Hyde (Zaxby's) flagged the campaign processing lag as a specific operational frustration: "One thing that's a little bit tricky is the data on the dashboard is real-time, but the campaign data isn't real-time. We'll go into the log to be like, okay, we knew 5 million people are supposed to get this campaign — we'll go into the log and see that 5,000 people were gifted that. Just having a very clear 'X people were targeted, here's how many we processed the campaign to today' — so you're not having to dig through logs to find that out." Maddie (Luna Grill) also raised a specific ask around A/B testing visibility for loyalty offers: "Next month we want to do a B test to see which offers will work most effectively for guests who have lapsed 120 and 180 days." The ability to measure offer-level performance within a loyalty context — not just campaign-level open rates — is a clear product gap.

7. 🏪 Loyalty as a Frontline Conversation Tool
Bobby (Graeter's) offered a perspective that reframes loyalty ROI beyond just marketing metrics — loyalty data is a tool for store-level engagement and manager motivation.

"We're in a world where transaction count is down. But it's very encouraging when we are looking at redemption rates and participation rate and check-ins — our managers feel more motivated and excited to talk about the loyalty program. It's a good way for them to understand that our loyalty guests come in more often and spend more money. So that motivates them to talk about the loyalty program and convert non-loyalty guests to loyalty guests." This highlights a non-obvious ROI dimension: loyalty analytics that are surfaced at the store level don't just measure program health — they drive enrollment behavior by motivating frontline staff.

Bottom line: Loyalty ROI is universally important but almost universally hard to prove. The core measurement gaps are: (1) no clean non-loyalty baseline to compare against, (2) no reliable incrementality methodology without proper control groups, (3) campaign feedback loops that are delayed or require manual digging, and (4) active rate definitions that vary by brand and don't reflect industry benchmarks. The brands closest to answering the ROI question — Zaxby's and Condado Tacos — have invested heavily in external CDPs and custom data infrastructure to get there.
